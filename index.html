Proposition: Precinct 75 Has a Disproportionately High Complaint Rate, Reflecting a Systemic Problem Rather Than Just a Few Bad Officers

FOR the Proposition

Precincts with more complaints also show higher rates per officer. Precinct 75 stands out as a clear outlier with 715 complaints across 327 officers. Source: NYPD Civilian Complaint Review Board, ProPublica, 1985–2020.

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • (Score +1.5) Plotting complaints per officer on the y-axis rather than raw complaint counts: Normalizing by the officer count reframes the proposition from "big precincts have more complaints" (which would be the usually expected idea) to "something about these precincts produces more complaints per person assigned there," which is a much greater institutional argument.
  • (Score −1) Adding a linear regression trend line without noting that there is a heavy influence from Precinct 75 being an outlier: Precinct 75’s sports a hefty 715 complaints, which is approximately double the next highest precinct. As a result of this fact, the trend line is pulled upward and its slope is steepened, making the positive relationship look stronger and more consistent than it would be if computed on the remaining 78 precincts alone.
  • (Score +1.5) Starting the y-axis at 0: Starting the y-axis at 0 leaves this sort of large empty region beneath the data, which compresses the visible spread and makes the trend line appear steeper relative to the data than it actually is. A viewer looking at the chart would perceive a stronger relationship between complaint volume and rate per officer than supported by the data alone.
  • (Score −0.5) Annotating only Precinct 75: By only calling out the greatest outlier by name, we focus the viewer’s attention onto the case that best supports the proposition, while leaving the rest of the data anonymous and harder to dissect individually.

AGAINST the Proposition

Top 15 officers in Precinct 75 by total complaint count. The y-axis begins at 20, and only the highest-complaint officers from the highest-complaint precinct are shown.

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • (Score −2) The y-axis starts at 20 rather than 0, which exaggerates the visual difference between bars. Officers with 25+ complaints appear far more severe than they actually are — the truncated axis makes a difference of a few complaints look dramatic, when in reality the gap between the best and worst officer shown is only about 10 complaints.
  • (Score −2) Precinct 75 was deliberately selected because it has the highest complaint count of any precinct. Focusing on it makes individual officer complaint numbers appear alarming, when officers in most other precincts would have significantly fewer complaints and the chart would look far less extreme.
  • (Score −1.5) Only the 15 officers with the most complaints are shown out of roughly 327 in the precinct. While this avoids a cluttered chart, it strips away the context of the full officer population and makes the complaint numbers seem much worse across the board than they truly are.
  • (Score +1) The bars are color-graded from darker (worst) to lighter (best), drawing mild attention to the higher-complaint officers without being outright misleading. This would have been far more deceptive if contrasting colors such as red versus green had been used, or if the gradient difference had been made more drastic.

Final Reflection

Before starting this project, we collectively weren't aware just how many standard and ordinary design choices would end up sitting somewhere in the gray area between honest and manipulative. Deciding which precincts to include, how to word a title, whether to call out a specific data point — all feel like regular calls to make, but each one subtly alters what a viewer sees and walks away believing. The more our group sat with those decisions, the more we came to understand that the line between making a chart readable and directing a reader toward a conclusion is much, much blurrier than we initially gave it credit for.

When working on the FOR visualization, we found that the most effectively persuasive choices weren't the loud ones. Normalizing complaint counts by officer size felt like a transparent and rigorous analytical choice — and in many ways it was — but it also had the effect of making the proposition harder to fact-check, since viewers would have to reconstruct the original data themselves to push back on it. What that taught us is that the most effective persuasion in data visualization isn't a manipulated axis or a misleading color scheme, but rather an analytical decision that looks solid and reasonable on the surface yet has already done the work of steering the viewer before they've processed anything. That realization changed how we think about what it means to visualize data ethically.
